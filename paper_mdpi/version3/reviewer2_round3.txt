We understand the point of the reviewer, but the requested feature is simply out of the scope of this project (and hardly compatible with the timing required by the publishing process). We could, in theory, implement a very basic calibration routine (e.g. asking the user to input the calibration points, then tune a linear model and interpolate all the pixels). But given the state of the art, this function will be scarcely used. For example, many modern cameras have dedicated calibration routines that leverage the embedded camera's sensor (e.g. the MicaSense's image processing library) For that, it is best to use the camera's software. Moreover, other existing suites like OpenDroneMap already cover the calibration aspects. We are not competing with this kind of well established software: our niche application - a niche for which we found justification - is for agricultural experiments, which are typically organized in plots, and require per-plot analyses. Our software doesn't aim to become a total pipeline substitution (from raw images to final report). In our designated place in the chain of execution, calibration already happened.

- This is not the appropriate place to discuss technically what drone phenotyping does or does not entail, but authors' response downplays a central methodological aspect of UAV phenotyping: radiometric calibration using reflectance panels. Numerous studies show that, without this calibration, vegetation indices are not comparable across dates, sensors, or lighting conditions, directly impacting their validity and reproducibility. The title of this article, "DRONE2REPORT: A Configuration-Driven Multi-Sensor Pipeline for UAV-Based Crop Phenotyping and Precision Agriculture," mentions phenotyping as a goal, so it would be inappropriate to exclude these aspects from the scope of this work… At the very least, this should be acknowledged, clarified, and discussed in the manuscript so that the reader can understand the proposed software's place within the workflow.

- On the other hand, it's only now becoming clearer that DRONE2REPORT comes "after" photogrammetric solutions that allow for the initial geoprocessing of drone data, including radiometric calibration. But then, the article's title clearly overstates the true capabilities of the proposed solution. Finally, the timing and urgency of the publications are understandable, we all have them, but it cannot be used as an argument at this stage.

As long as the input data comes in the form of a .tif image with the proper embedded information (e.g. the CRS) plus accessory files (.shp .cpg .dbf .shx .prj) the software doesn't make any distinction. Orthophotos are very rarely (almost never) used in agricultural surveying processes with drones; photos captured by UAVs without orthorectification are used in very few applications; so, the vast majority of applications related to plant phenotyping and precision agriculture use orthomosaics.

In general terms, and in response to this third opportunity for review (following two Major Revisions):

- The manuscript presents DRONE2REPORT as a “configuration-driven multi-sensor pipeline” capable of integrating sensors, automating analysis, and facilitating UAV phenotyping in a reproducible and accessible manner. However, exhaustive analysis shows that the implemented solution is considerably more limited: a sequential task executor in a CLI environment that operates exclusively on pre-generated orthomosaics, without intervening in key pipeline stages such as acquisition, photogrammetry, or radiometric calibration. In particular, in addition to what has already been mentioned in previous responses, the so-called “multi-sensor” approach is limited to the passive integration of already core-registered data through reprojection and interpolation, without developing its own alignment or fusion methods.

- In this context, there is a clear disproportion between the broad scope suggested by the title and narrative of the article and the actual nature of the tool, which corresponds more precisely to a configurable batch processing engine for plot analysis than to a complete tool for phenotyping and precision agriculture. Specifically, the article title should accurately reflect the tool's actual capabilities.

- Furthermore, and also based on the clarifications provided by the authors in this third presentation, the comparisons made in the Discussion section appear somewhat biased. For example, the comparison with ODM/WebODM is rhetorically useful but methodologically weak: it constructs an artificial dichotomy (reconstruction vs. analysis) that does not accurately reflect the current state of the ecosystem, since the ecosystem itself (especially WebODM) includes raster analysis, index calculation, and visualization tools. Consequently, rather than clearly justifying DRONE2REPORT's niche, it introduces a bias that tends to exaggerate its relative contribution.

- Therefore, the authors are asked to exercise extreme caution regarding the aspects indicated in this review and in the comments made in the two attached files. As you will surely understand, a scientific publication cannot raise disproportionate or inaccurate statements in order to highlight its own work/output, and this is also inappropriate if it is a technical note and not a research article.
